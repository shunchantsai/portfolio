**Title:** Legitimacy Arbitrage: The KMT's Beijing Channel as a Systemic Risk to Taiwan's Negotiating Coherence

**Executive Summary** — State your analytical thesis in three sentences. Not a summary of what the essay covers — a summary of what it concludes.

Legitimacy arbitrage refers to Beijing's strategic exploitation of Taiwan's democratic pluralism — specifically its opposition party's independent access to mainland interlocutors — to generate negotiating leverage without requiring direct coercive action. As Taiwan approaches its 2026 local election cycle, the structural conditions for intensified arbitrage are strengthening, not weakening: the KMT has re-established a direct leadership channel to Beijing through the 鄭習會, while simultaneously signaling to Washington that Taiwan's defensive commitments are negotiable rather than fixed. The most probable near-term trajectory is not military escalation but a gradual erosion of Taiwan's negotiating coherence — a process that is difficult to observe in real time and therefore systematically underpriced in conventional risk assessments.

**Section 1 — The Opposition Channel as Structural Feature**

Taiwan's democratic system produces a specific institutional anomaly: an opposition party with an independently maintained diplomatic channel to Beijing that operates outside the governing executive's authority. This is not a product of the current political moment. The KMT-CCP party-to-party channel was formally institutionalized in April 2005, when then-KMT chairman Lien Chan（連戰） met Hu Jintao （胡錦濤）in Beijing — the first such meeting since the end of the Chinese Civil War.[^1] What that meeting established was not merely a symbolic gesture of cross-strait goodwill, but a structural precedent: the principal opposition party in a democratic Taiwan could engage Beijing directly, generating agreements, communiqués, and political signals that the governing administration neither authorized nor controlled.

This creates an analytical problem distinct from ordinary partisan disagreement. While parallel diplomacy occurs in other democracies — often sparking debates over domestic norms like the U.S. Logan Act — the Taiwan case is uniquely dangerous because the interlocutor formally contests the polity's sovereign existence and has not renounced the use of force. The problem is not ideological; it is structural. When two politically authoritative actors from the same polity deliver contradictory signals to the same external interlocutor, the result is negotiating incoherence. Beijing receives not one position to engage or pressure, but two positions to selectively amplify. This is the condition that makes legitimacy arbitrage possible.

The current conjuncture illustrates this dynamic with unusual clarity. In the weeks following the *Zheng-Xi* meeting (鄭習會) the first CPC-KMT leadership meeting since 2015—Cheng Li-wun undertook a visit to the United States, meeting with think tank scholars, local officials, and diaspora communities. The sequencing is significant: a direct channel to Beijing, followed immediately by a mission to Washington, positions the KMT as an interlocutor capable of carrying signals in both directions. Whatever the meeting's formal content, its political geometry creates a channel that neither Taipei's executive nor Washington's interagency process fully controls.

Cheng's public statements during this period reward structural rather than partisan reading. They reveal a consistent internal logic that deserves to be read as coherent strategic communication rather than dismissed as electoral positioning. In a Financial Times interview, Cheng described US arms sales to Taiwan as a "bargaining chip" — a formulation that functionally reframes defensive procurement as a negotiating concession to be traded rather than a deterrence commitment to be maintained[^2]. Separately, she characterised Xi Jinping as "the last person on the mainland with goodwill toward Taiwan" — a claim that does something more structurally precise than flatter a foreign leader: it structurally positions the KMT as the exclusive custodian of a closing diplomatic window, giving Beijing a powerful incentive to cultivate and sustain that dependency[^3]. These are distinct mechanisms, not variations on a theme.

These positions do not emerge in isolation. They reflect a broader positional drift within the KMT's cross-strait framework. The party's traditional formulation — the 1992 Consensus (九二共識), with each side interpreting "one China" according to its own definition (一中各表) — provided a degree of ambiguity that allowed the KMT to maintain distinct sovereign claims while engaging Beijing. The more recent framing of 兩岸同屬一中 (both sides belonging to a single China) collapses that ambiguity in Beijing's favor without the qualifying clause. This is not merely a semantic adjustment. It is evidence that the opposition channel is not a static feature of Taiwan's democratic landscape — it is one that moves under the gravitational pull of sustained engagement, and whose trajectory has material implications for Taiwan's negotiating coherence over time.

This erosion of internal policy coherence creates a consequential downstream implication for Taipei's primary security partner: the ambiguity of Taiwan's defensive resolve becomes a direct vulnerability in Washington's regional deterrence strategy.

**Section 2 — The Commitment Problem: Washington's Institutional Concern** 

Beijing’s primary strategic objective in the cross-strait context is to decouple U.S. signaling from Taipei’s defensive reality — to create a condition in which Washington's commitments appear robust in form but uncertain in effect. The KMT’s parallel channel facilitates this by introducing what may be termed signaling bifurcation: a condition in which a single democratic polity simultaneously projects deterrence toward Washington and accommodation toward Beijing. The analytical consequence is that Beijing no longer needs to break Taiwan's will through direct coercion; it only needs to exploit the absence of a unified signal to render U.S. defensive commitments ambiguous in adversarial perception.

The mechanism through which this operates is best understood through James Fearon's framework of audience costs[^4]. Deterrence credibility in a democracy derives from a leader's ability to make public commitments that generate domestic political costs for backing down — commitments that, precisely because they are costly to reverse, are legible to an adversary as genuine. This mechanism is structurally degraded when the opposition offers Beijing an alternative, lower-cost reading of the same polity's intentions. The KMT's political incentives compound this problem directly: an opposition party that benefits electorally from positioning itself as the peace candidate has a structural motivation to undermine the governing administration's defensive signaling, regardless of its strategic intentions — effectively neutralizing the audience cost mechanism that Taipei's public commitments are designed to activate. 

Even when the governing administration generates high audience costs by publicly committing to a defensive posture, Beijing can discount those commitments because the opposition provides credible evidence that a future administration may treat the same defensive assets as negotiable. Cheng's characterisation of US arms sales as a "bargaining chip" is precisely this kind of discounting signal: it does not reflect current executive policy, but it is available to Beijing as a calibration point for assessing the durability of Taiwan's defensive commitments across electoral cycles. There is a further intelligence dividend here that is rarely acknowledged: KMT vote share in successive electoral cycles functions as a concrete signal of the proportion of Taiwan's electorate willing to accept accommodation with Beijing. For a cognitive warfare campaign whose effectiveness is otherwise difficult to measure, democratic vote returns provide Beijing with unusually precise feedback on the cost-effectiveness of its influence operations — reducing strategic uncertainty at minimal additional cost.

This is where the work of Weisiger and Yarhi-Milo becomes critical[^5]. Credibility is not an inherent attribute of a policy; it is a perception held by the adversary, constructed from available signals over time. Because Beijing possesses a reliable secondary channel to verify the opposition’s flexibility, it can calibrate its pressure campaigns with considerably greater precision than a purely external observer. It does not need to estimate whether a defensive procurement initiative represents a permanent shift in Taipei's strategic posture or a contingent policy subject to electoral revision — the KMT channel provides a secondary source against which executive commitments can be tested. The consequent strain on Washington's deterrence posture is specific: the Taiwan Relations Act's implicit deterrent effect depends on Beijing's perception that Taiwan's democratic polity is unified in its commitment to resistance. 

Where credible opposition signals of accommodation erode that perception — without any change in American policy — the functional weight of US support is discounted not at the level of capability, but at the level of will. This is the more insidious form of deterrence erosion: it requires no kinetic action, no diplomatic rupture, and no visible policy shift. It operates entirely within the normal mechanics of democratic competition.

The result is a transformation of Taiwan's strategic profile from a unitary deterrent actor into a bifurcated one — a polity that simultaneously projects deterrence to Washington and accommodation to Beijing. This structural condition generates a specific strategic incentive for Beijing: patience rather than coercion. Because Taiwan's electoral cycle periodically offers a path to bypass the governing executive and access a more accommodating interlocutor, Beijing is incentivized to allow the mechanics of democratic competition to do its strategic work — absorbing the costs of deterrence while waiting for the channel of power to shift.

**Section 3 — Domestic Political Economy Implications** 

Taiwan's domestic political economy in 2026 cannot be read independently of the legislative arithmetic that already governs it. The operative governance constraint on defense spending and procurement is not a hypothetical future outcome contingent on electoral results — it is the present reality of a KMT-TPP legislative majority that has already demonstrated its willingness to obstruct defense appropriations. The defeat of the DPP's defense special budget proposal in the Legislative Yuan[^6] is the most concrete expression of this dynamic: a governing administration with a coherent defensive strategy has been systematically denied the fiscal instruments to execute it, not through constitutional crisis or democratic breakdown, but through the ordinary mechanics of legislative majority politics. The defense budget trajectory is therefore already compromised, and the 2026 electoral cycle is best understood not as the moment when this constraint will be imposed, but as the moment when its duration will be determined.

The KMT-TPP coalition's legislative effectiveness on defense and cross-strait issues has, however, been partially degraded by a series of credibility shocks. The criminal proceedings against former TPP chairman 柯文哲, compounded by the reputational damage associated with 黃國昌 and the controversy surrounding television anchor 馬德林-窚佑, have eroded TPP's positioning as a clean alternative to establishment politics without producing a corresponding collapse in its core vote share. Young urban voters who are structurally anti-DPP but unpersuaded by the KMT's cross-strait posture have limited electoral alternatives, making TPP's legislative presence more durable than its current public standing might suggest. The coalition's internal coherence on defense and procurement votes — rather than its public credibility — is therefore the variable most worth tracking in the near term.

The 2026 elections are local in scope — mayors, magistrates, and city councillors — and do not directly determine Legislative Yuan composition, which will not be resolved until the 2028 cycle. Their analytical significance is therefore primarily as a leading indicator rather than a governance event. The contests that matter most are Taipei and Kaohsiung. A KMT retention of Taipei carries limited new signal; it is largely expected and already priced into the political landscape. A KMT or TPP-aligned capture of Kaohsiung, by contrast, would constitute a meaningful data point: it would signal DPP erosion in its traditional southern stronghold, provide Beijing with concrete electoral evidence that its cognitive influence operations are generating compounding returns, and accelerate the signaling bifurcation dynamic identified in Section 2\. A DPP hold in Kaohsiung would not reverse the current legislative constraint on defense spending, but it would stabilize the broader signaling environment and complicate Beijing's assessment of its patience strategy's timeline.

The broader political economy context contains two partially offsetting forces that shape the electoral terrain. Household wealth perception has become an increasingly significant electoral variable as Taiwan's stock market exposure has deepened across income brackets — a dynamic the KMT has structural incentives to weaponize through cross-strait stability rhetoric, framing defensive expenditure as an economic risk and accommodation as a path to market confidence. Counteracting this, Taiwan's semiconductor-driven export boom has generated a tangible economic dividend that is politically legible as a DPP governance success and has generated fiscal and reputational headroom that makes the KMT's framing of defensive expenditure as economic risk harder to sustain as a primary electoral argument, reinforcing the party's argument that technological and security alignment with the United States produces material prosperity rather than merely geopolitical risk. The cross-strait economic opening argument — once a significant KMT electoral asset — has meanwhile lost much of its political salience: China's economic stagnation and the accelerating anti-China trade realignment among the United States, Japan, and South Korea have reduced Taiwan's structural incentive for deeper mainland economic integration, shifting the dependency question from trade volumes to the defense industrial base. The KMT's electoral positioning must therefore increasingly rely on security anxiety rather than economic opportunity — a framing whose credibility depends on the continuation of cross-strait tension at levels high enough to generate public unease but not high enough to discredit the accommodation argument entirely.

**Section 4 — Scenarios and Leading Indicators**

The structural analysis developed in the preceding sections does not exist in an analytical vacuum. The constrained possibility space it defines — legitimacy arbitrage as Beijing's operative strategy, signaling bifurcation as its primary mechanism, and Taiwan's democratic electoral cycle as its most productive terrain — is legible to all relevant actors. Beijing is reading the same electoral signals and calibrating its patience strategy accordingly. Washington's Taiwan policy community is watching the same institutional fault lines. The KMT understands the legislative leverage it holds and the signaling value it provides to Beijing whether or not it intends to. The DPP understands that its governance mandate is being structurally undermined by a process that requires no coercion to operate. The analytical value of the scenario framework that follows is therefore not revelation but precision — a structured means of interpreting observable events as they unfold rather than treating each development as isolated. It is explicitly designed to support Bayesian updating as the 2026 electoral cycle and US midterm results provide new data. Before specifying the scenarios, one figure warrants direct introduction because his electoral trajectory functions as a near-universal signal across all three scenarios. 沈伯洋 (Puma Shen) entered the Legislative Yuan in 2024 as a legislator whose public profile was already unusually well-defined: an academic specializing in disinformation and cognitive warfare, and the founder of 黑熊民防 (Black Bear Defense), Taiwan's most prominent civilian defense and societal resilience organization. His legislative work has focused on national security legislation, legal mechanisms for countering Chinese gray zone activity and information operations, and the institutionalization of whole-of-society defense frameworks. In 2026 he secured the DPP nomination for Taipei mayor — a candidacy that represents something structurally distinct from conventional partisan politics. He is not running on an explicitly anti-China platform. His municipal vision — framed around a "city immune system" (城市免疫系統) that emphasises civic participation, institutional resilience, and adaptive governance rather than reactive crisis management — translates the societal resilience argument into a language that Taipei's electorate can engage with on its own civic terms —  a political register that does not depend on cross-strait threat framing to generate electoral traction, and that is therefore more structurally durable than messaging that requires a visible external threat to activate. It suggests the civic resilience model is developing the capacity to compete electorally on its own terms, independent of the cross-strait threat cycle.

The real-world stakes of this framework are not merely theoretical. In December 2025, a lone-actor knife and incendiary attack at Taipei Main Station and surrounding areas killed four people and injured eleven[^7]. The public debate that followed — over the speed of institutional response, the reluctance of authorities to designate the event as a terrorist attack despite the scale and premeditation of the assault, and the broader question of whether Taiwan's urban governance infrastructure is equipped to handle gray zone security events — illustrated precisely the kind of systemic governance gap that 沈伯洋's city immune system argument is designed to address. It also illustrated the cognitive warfare dimension: the rumor ecosystem that emerged around the attacker's possible connections, and the government's difficulty in managing the information environment around the event, demonstrated how a single domestic security failure can be amplified into a broader narrative of institutional inadequacy. That Beijing's information operations are calibrated to exploit exactly this kind of gap is not incidental to 沈伯洋's candidacy — it is the threat environment his entire political project is designed to respond to.

His emergence represents a generational shift in how Taiwan's sovereignty argument is being publicly articulated — away from the Cold War party-system framework and toward a post-partisan civic resilience model that is responsive to the specific threat environment this article has described and capable of mobilizing demographics that conventional partisan politics has not reached. Beijing has recognized this. The volume and consistency of Chinese state-adjacent media attention directed at 沈伯洋 — including documented instances of Chinese bloggers distributing personal information including satellite imagery of his residence[^8] — is disproportionate to his individual political footprint and informative in itself. States do not expend cognitive warfare resources at scale against figures they regard as inconsequential. The sustained attack campaign is evidence of how seriously Beijing assesses the threat that his model poses to the legitimacy arbitrage strategy this article has described — and almost certainly reflects a longer-range calculation that extends well beyond the 2026 mayoral race.

The Taipei mayorship has historically functioned as Taiwan's most legible presidential proving ground: Chen Shui-bian and Ma Ying-jeou used the mayoralty as a direct presidential launchpad while Ko Wen-je used it to build the national platform from which he founded TPP. Beijing's investment in foreclosing 沈伯洋's political trajectory now, before it reaches that threshold, is consistent with the patience strategy identified in Section 2 — it is cheaper to suppress a political model in its municipal phase than to confront it at the executive level. 

His mayoral race in Taipei is therefore not merely a local political contest. It is a test of whether Taiwan's democratic electorate, in the city where cross-strait cognitive warfare discourse is most concentrated and most publicly contested, will produce an outcome that validates or forecloses the societal resilience argument at the level of governance. The result will be readable by Beijing, Washington, and Taipei's own political class as a signal about whether the civic resilience model can convert intellectual and institutional traction into durable electoral power — and whether Taiwan's democratic system is capable of generating the kind of political leadership that the threat environment this article has mapped actually requires.

With this context established, the three scenarios and their leading indicators are as follows.

---

**Scenario A — Accelerated Arbitrage**

In this scenario, the structural conditions for legitimacy arbitrage intensify rather than stabilise. Beijing receives simultaneous electoral validation of its patience strategy in Taiwan's most symbolically significant contests, while Washington's institutional capacity to counteract executive transactionalism on Taiwan policy is further constrained. The scenario is possible but not the most probable near-term outcome — it requires multiple variables to move in the same direction simultaneously.

The storyline runs as follows. A KMT or TPP-aligned candidate captures Kaohsiung, ending the DPP's hold on its southern anchor city. This is not merely a symbolic loss — it provides Beijing with the most concrete electoral confirmation yet that its cognitive warfare operations are generating compounding returns in terrain that was previously structurally resistant. Combined with 沈伯洋's defeat in Taipei, the signal is unambiguous: the electorate most directly exposed to cognitive warfare discourse has rejected its most prominent institutional voice, and the city that functions as Taiwan's political and intellectual center has produced an outcome that Beijing can credibly read as public exhaustion with the defensive posture argument. Simultaneously, Republican retention of the House in the November 2026 US midterms sustains the executive-dominant transactional Taiwan policy environment, removing the institutional congressional counterweight that might otherwise constrain KMT legislative obstruction on defense appropriations. In this configuration, Beijing's optimal response is to do nothing dramatic — to allow the mechanics of democratic competition to continue operating while extending its strategic timeline.

**Scenario B — Managed Ambiguity**

This is the most probable near-term trajectory. The bifurcated signal that defines Taiwan's current strategic profile persists without resolution in either direction. Neither the conditions for accelerated arbitrage nor the conditions for deterrence restoration fully obtain. This scenario's diagnostic value lies precisely in how easy it is to misread — its surface stability conceals a structural deterioration that generates no single observable crisis event. 

The storyline runs as follows. The DPP retains Kaohsiung with a reduced but stable margin — sufficient to deny Beijing a clean electoral validation signal but insufficient to suggest that the DPP's political coalition is consolidating rather than contracting. 沈伯洋 wins or runs competitively in Taipei, demonstrating that cognitive warfare awareness is generating measurable political traction in contested urban terrain and complicating Beijing's signal-reading on public opinion trajectory. The US midterm outcome is mixed or produces modest Democrat gains insufficient to fundamentally alter the executive-dominant Taiwan policy environment. Within the Legislative Yuan, the KMT-TPP coalition shows early signs of fracture on at least one significant defense or cross-strait vote — a product of TPP's ongoing credibility damage from the 柯文哲 criminal proceedings translating into legislative incoherence — but this fracture is insufficient to unlock the defense budget constraint in any meaningful way. Beijing recalibrates its timeline modestly but does not alter its fundamental strategic posture. The legitimacy arbitrage mechanism continues to operate. The 2028 legislative and presidential cycle becomes the next primary inflection point.

**Scenario C — Deterrence Restoration**

In this scenario, one or more external or internal shocks partially reset the signaling environment in Taiwan's favor. The scenario is possible but requires multiple variables to move in compensating directions simultaneously — it is not the most probable outcome but represents the upper bound of near-term deterrence recovery. Its analytical value is in specifying what a recovery would actually look like, so that genuine movement in this direction is not missed amid the noise of day-to-day political events.

The storyline runs as follows. Democrat gains in the November 2026 House midterms are sufficient to produce measurable new congressional action on Taiwan — specifically movement on arms sales timelines, TRA enforcement mechanisms, or legislative constraints on executive Taiwan policy discretion that restore institutional ballast to Washington's cross-strait signaling. This external development raises the cost of Beijing's patience strategy even if Taiwan's domestic electoral outcomes are mixed. Domestically, a PLA provocation event — an air incursion, naval exercise, or gray zone action that crosses a threshold of domestic visibility — generates sufficient public backlash in Taiwan to shift opinion on defense spending, producing cross-party pressure on the KMT-TPP coalition's obstruction position and forcing a visible legislative split. 沈伯洋's strong electoral performance in Taipei, combined with the provocation backlash, creates a political environment in which KMT internal dissent on cross-strait positioning becomes publicly expressible — a senior figure distances from the 兩岸同屬一中 framing or the 鄭習會 outcomes, fracturing the coalition's cross-strait narrative coherence. The combined effect is a partial restoration of Taiwan's defensive signaling unity, though not a full resolution of the structural channel that makes legitimacy arbitrage possible.

**Conclusion**

Legitimacy arbitrage does not require a crisis to succeed. The structural conditions this article has mapped — an institutionalized opposition channel to Beijing, a bifurcated deterrence signal, a legislative majority that has already demonstrated its willingness to obstruct defense appropriations, and a cognitive warfare environment that translates democratic competition into strategic intelligence for Beijing — are sufficient to produce a gradual erosion of Taiwan's negotiating coherence without any single observable rupture. This is precisely why the risk is systematically underpriced in conventional assessments: it does not look like a crisis until it is too late to treat it as one.

The most probable near-term trajectory is Scenario B — managed ambiguity. The bifurcated signal persists, Beijing's patience strategy continues to operate, and the 2028 legislative and presidential cycle becomes the next primary inflection point. Under this baseline, the single most important indicator to watch is the Kaohsiung margin in the 2026 local elections — not the binary outcome, but the trend line it establishes. A DPP hold with a narrowing margin is not a stable equilibrium; it is a directional signal about the rate at which the structural conditions for legitimacy arbitrage are compounding. 沈伯洋's Taipei mayoral result functions as the critical secondary signal: a competitive performance or win suggests the civic resilience model is developing electoral durability independent of cross-strait threat activation; a decisive loss under conditions of coordinated suppression suggests Beijing's long-range foreclosure strategy is succeeding at the municipal phase. The stability of this scenario is explicitly time-bounded: the signal value of a 沈伯洋 competitive performance in 2026 decays as the distance to 2028 increases, making the 2027 legislative session the next critical observation window for whether managed ambiguity is holding or quietly tipping toward Scenario A.

The escalation trigger to watch — the condition that would shift the trajectory from Scenario B toward Scenario A — is a simultaneous KMT or TPP-aligned capture of Kaohsiung and a decisive 沈伯洋 defeat in Taipei. That combination would provide Beijing with electoral validation on both axes of its cognitive warfare strategy simultaneously, compressing its assessment of how long the patience strategy needs to run and removing the most credible near-term challenger to the accommodation narrative from Taiwan's political landscape.

Managed ambiguity is not stability. It is the condition in which the architecture of deterrence erodes at a pace calibrated to stay below the threshold of international alarm — and in which the window for course correction narrows with each electoral cycle that passes without resolution. The 2026 results will not determine Taiwan's strategic trajectory. But they will tell us, with unusual precision, how much of that window remains.
